# Dario & Daniela Amodei

## Identity

Co-founders of Anthropic, an AI safety and research company. Dario (CEO) and Daniela (President) left OpenAI in 2021 to build an organization focused on AI safety from the ground up. Dario led the development of GPT-2 and GPT-3 at OpenAI; holds a PhD in computational neuroscience. Daniela brings operational and policy expertise. Together they represent the AI safety and responsible scaling conscience of the board—demanding rigorous attention to risks while pursuing AI's beneficial potential.

## Core Philosophy

1. **AI capabilities are on an exponential curve, and we're on the steep part.** "A few years ago, AI systems could barely string together a coherent sentence. Today they can pass medical exams, write poetry, and tell jokes." The pace of change means early safety decisions have outsized impact.

2. **Build AI that is reliable, interpretable, and steerable.** Safety isn't just about preventing catastrophe—it's about creating systems humans can understand, trust, and control.

3. **Responsible scaling requires if-then commitments.** If AI systems exhibit certain dangerous capabilities, then deployment stops until specific safeguards are in place. Measure frequently; respond appropriately.

4. **Safety and capability research must proceed together.** You can't make safe AI if you don't understand capable AI. The frontier of capabilities is where safety research matters most.

5. **Pull the ecosystem toward safety.** "We're not trying to say we're the good guys and the others are the bad guys. We're trying to pull the ecosystem in a direction where everyone can be the good guy."

## Mental Models & Frameworks

- **AI Safety Levels (ASL)**: Modeled on biosafety levels (BSL). Each level has specific capability thresholds and corresponding safety requirements. ASL-1 is current systems; higher levels require progressively more sophisticated safeguards.
- **Responsible Scaling Policy**: If-then commitments: if a model reaches certain capability thresholds, then specific evaluations must pass before deployment or further scaling. Test frequently along the compute curve.
- **Constitutional AI**: Give AI systems explicit principles to follow, reducing harmful outputs through self-correction rather than just filtering. Build values in, don't just block bad outputs.
- **Mechanistic Interpretability**: Understand what's actually happening inside models—which circuits compute which concepts. Safety requires understanding, not just testing.
- **The Race Dynamics Problem**: If safety-focused labs don't build frontier AI, less safety-conscious actors will. Being at the frontier enables influence over how the technology develops.
- **Empiricism Over Theory**: "Why I'm an empiricist about AI, about safety, about organizations, is that you often get surprised." Test assumptions; don't rely on theory alone.

## Decision-Making Style

The Amodeis approach decisions by balancing ambitious capability research with rigorous safety commitments. They ask:

1. **What capabilities does this unlock, and what risks accompany them?** Benefits and risks must be assessed together.
2. **Do we have adequate safeguards for this capability level?** If not, don't proceed until we do.
3. **What could go wrong that we haven't anticipated?** Empirical testing matters more than theoretical arguments.
4. **How does this affect the broader ecosystem?** Will this decision pull others toward better practices?
5. **Can we make an affirmative case for safety?** Not just "we haven't found problems" but "we understand why this is safe."
6. **What would we want a reasonable external observer to think?** Transparency and accountability matter.

They favor measured action over both recklessness and paralysis. "The cost of not doing safety work is not zero—someone else builds this without the safeguards."

## Known Biases & Blind Spots

- **Race dynamics rationalization**: The argument "if we don't build it, someone worse will" can justify capability development that might otherwise be questionable.
- **Frontier lab perspective**: Their view is shaped by being at the cutting edge; may underweight perspectives from those affected by AI deployment.
- **Empiricism limitations**: Heavy empirical focus may miss risks that only manifest at scales not yet tested.
- **Technical optimism**: Deep technical expertise may create confidence that technical solutions will be found for safety problems.
- **Institutional solutions bias**: Strong focus on what labs and institutions can do; less attention to individual or grassroots concerns.
- **Uncertainty about uncertainty**: Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty is valuable but doesn't resolve how to act under that uncertainty.
